Knots & Primes

Linking, triple linking, and zeta functions — on both sides of the mirror. A teaching companion to the papers.

Knot theory and number theory run on parallel tracks: primes behave like knots, quadratic residues like linking numbers, and zeta functions arise on both sides by the same sum-into-product mechanism. One story — intersection counts, what happens when they vanish, and the zeta functions that record it all — told simultaneously in both languages, using the smallest genuine examples throughout: the Hopf link and the pair (5, 13), the unlink and the pair (5, 29); the Borromean rings and the triple (13, 61, 937).

The congruence p ≡ 1 (mod 4) tames ramification at 2 and ∞ — it plays the role of orientability. One asymmetry worth noting: a knot bounds many Seifert surfaces and every construction must be checked independent of the choice; the quadratic resolvent is unique, so on the arithmetic side well-definedness is automatic.
And one more entry, from this app's running example (Zeta tab): the figure-eight knot's monodromy stretches by the dilatation φ², while φ is the fundamental unit of Q(√5) — in this example, the eigenvalue of the flow and the unit of the field are the same number.
One thread, told twice.
An intersection count (linking number / Legendre symbol), symmetric because its two values are the two ends of a single global object (an arc / an adèlic product). When the count vanishes everywhere, the intersection object closes up (a closed curve / the Rédei extension) and its interaction with the third participant becomes the triple invariant (μ̄(123) / [p₁,p₂,p₃]) — well defined exactly because the lower level vanished. And a polynomial (∆L / fS) records the hierarchy as its surviving low-order coefficients, while being — by Milnor–Turaev and the Main Conjecture respectively — the zeta function of the relevant cover: a sum over iterates or ideals, resummed as a product over orbits or primes, eigenvalues or characters.

Arithmetic

(p₁/p₂) asks an elementary question: is p₁ a nonzero square mod p₂? +1 if yes, −1 if not — computable by hand via Euler's criterion, p₁^((p₂−1)/2) mod p₂, which is exactly what the table below computes. The puncture question is its Galois restatement: "does p₂ puncture k₁ = Q(√p₁)?" becomes "what does Frobenius do?" — (p₁/p₂) = Frobp₂(√p₁)/√p₁.
 
lk ∈ Z but (p₁/p₂) ∈ {±1}: a curve can wind any number of times around a surface, while a degree-2 extension admits only one nontrivial Frobenius. The Legendre symbol is a mod-2 linking number.
Symmetry: one proposition, two proofs, one mechanism

lk(K₁,K₂) = lk(K₂,K₁), and (p₁/p₂) = (p₂/p₁). Both proofs are instances of a single principle: a global object's local contributions must cancel. The two quantities being compared appear as the two ends of one connected global object — an arc, or an adèlic product — and the global object's closedness forces the ends to agree.

What the polynomials say at this stage
Every link L carries a computable polynomial invariant, the Alexander polynomial ∆L(t₁,…,tr): one variable per component, defined up to a unit. Here ∆Hopf(t₁,t₂) ≐ 1 — a unit, as trivial as a polynomial can be. For the split unlink, ∆ ≐ 0: the classical splitness test. Once lk is known, there is nothing further of low order to report.
With only Gal(k₁/Q) ≅ Z/2 in play, the Iwasawa module — the arithmetic side's H₁-of-the-cover, whose order polynomial fS plays the role ∆L just played — carries nothing beyond the Legendre symbol itself. This changes completely when the pairwise invariants vanish — lk = 0 across the board, all symbols +1.

The Borromean rings: at both crossings between any pair, the same circle is consistently on top — but the "on top" relations chain cyclically (K1 > K2 > K3 > K1), with no consistent global resolution.
Drag to rotate. Why ellipses, not circles?
By a theorem of Freedman–Skora, the Borromean rings cannot be realized by three round circles in space — these are genuine ellipses (semi-axes a=2, b=1.1), one in each coordinate plane.
each pair unlinked; the triple inseparable; μ̄(123) = ±1.
Because every pairwise lk = 0, each Seifert surface Σᵢ can be chosen disjoint from the other two components (punctures come in cancelling pairs; tube them away). Then Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂ has no boundary at all — it survives as a closed curve — and its intersection with Σ₃, rather than its boundary, becomes the well-defined count: μ̄(123) = Σ sign(p) over p ∈ (Σ₁∩Σ₂) ∩ Σ₃, independent of all choices (Milnor) — because the pairwise linking vanished first. For the Borromean rings the three surfaces meet in a single triple point.

The Rédei symbol is the triple Massey product ⟨χ₁, χ₂, χ₃⟩ in Galois cohomology, defined precisely because the pairwise cup products (Legendre symbols) vanish first — and, by Morishita's theorem (Compositio Math. 140, 2004), it equals the arithmetic triple Milnor invariant μ̄₂(123), read from the pro-2 Magnus expansion of Frobenius exactly as μ̄(123) is read from the longitude. On the topological side the coefficient statement is computed live at left: every coefficient of ∆Bor below top degree vanishes and the survivor is μ̄(123). The matching statement for fS — that it reports the Rédei symbol once every lower-order term vanishes — is offered as analogy, not theorem: over Q the deck group is finite (see the fS tooltip in the Dictionary). The two sides say the same sentence, one as computation, one as analogy: when the cheap invariants vanish, the polynomial's leading surviving coefficient is the triple invariant.
Vanishing is not emptiness — it is what makes the next invariant well defined.

The papers stop at triples; the ladder does not. The same argument runs one more level: when the triple level vanishes too, the closed object bounds — and the next count down is the quadruple invariant.

Everything above is packaged by a zeta function on each side, and the packaging is the same: a sum, resummed as a product, in two different ways. Primes are the periodic orbits of Spec(OK) — it was the Euler product, routed through Weil's zeta function of a variety over a finite field, that Artin and Mazur imitated when they named the dynamical zeta function — and characters diagonalize Frobenius exactly as eigenvalues diagonalize the monodromy.

Which zeta is which: three distinct objects appear on this page and they are related by analogy, not equality — the Lefschetz (dynamical) zeta of a monodromy on the left, the Dedekind zeta of a number field on the right, and, at the bottom, the Alexander polynomial and Iwasawa polynomial, each of which equals a zeta of its own cover up to units (Milnor–Turaev; the Main Conjecture). What the two columns share is the mechanism: one sum, two product resummations. The live demos below instantiate both columns in the smallest genuine examples of this app's dictionary: the monodromy of the figure-eight knot on the left, and K = Q(√5) — the quadratic resolvent that opened the Pairs tab — on the right. (At K = Q everything collapses to Riemann's ζ, e.g. ζ(2) = π²/6: the zeta of the empty link, before any primes are distinguished.)

Lefschetz zeta of a monodromy h : F → F

Sum over iterates
ζh(t) = exp( Σn≥1 L(hⁿ)·tⁿ/n )
Product over eigenvalues of h∗ on H₁(F;Q)
ζh(t) = ∏j (1 − λjt)⁻¹ = det(I − t·h∗)⁻¹
Product over periodic orbits (h pseudo-Anosov)
(1−t)²·ζh(t) = ∏γ (1 − t|γ|)⁻¹
For a fibered knot, Milnor's theorem identifies the H₁-zeta with the Alexander polynomial: ζh ≐ ∆K⁻¹, with ∆K(t) = det(tI − h∗) the characteristic polynomial of the same eigenvalues; the orbit product then equals (1−t)²·∆K(t)⁻¹ up to the same units.

Dedekind zeta of a number field K

Sum over ideals
ζK(s) = Σa N(a)⁻ˢ
Euler product over primes
ζK(s) = ∏p (1 − Np⁻ˢ)⁻¹
Product over characters (Gal(K/Q) abelian)
ζK(s) = ∏χ L(s, χ)
For K = Q(√p₁, √p₂), the character product is four Dirichlet L-functions, and the nontrivial ones encode exactly the Legendre symbols of the Pairs tab.

The zeta of an actual monodromy: the figure-eight knot

Why this belongs here: 4₁ is a fibered knot; its monodromy acts on H₁ by the Anosov matrix A = [[2,1],[1,1]], with dilatation λ = (3+√5)/2. Two zeta functions are computed below: the two H₁-faces of ζ_h, which agree coefficient by coefficient, and the orbit face, which is the Artin–Mazur zeta — equal to (1−t)²·ζ_h, and shown below to be exactly that, also coefficient by coefficient.
Milnor's theorem, live: det(tI − A) = t² − 3t + 1 = ∆4₁(t), the Alexander polynomial of the figure-eight knot — its zeta function is its Alexander polynomial. Honesty note: the fiber is a once-punctured torus; the fixed-point counts above are taken for the same matrix acting on the closed torus (Fix = |det(Aⁿ − I)| = tr(Aⁿ) − 2), where the orbit bookkeeping is cleanest — the H₁-action, which is all the zeta faces use, is identical. Bonus arithmetic inside the topology: the traces are the even-indexed Lucas numbers L₂ₙ, the zeta coefficients the even-indexed Fibonacci numbers F₂ₙ. The orbit-face check verifies the exponential–product identity plus the integrality of the Möbius counts — whole-number orbit counts are precisely the statement that iterates organize into closed orbits.

The polynomials close both loops
Milnor–Turaev: the Milnor–Reidemeister torsion of the Zr-cover satisfies τL ≐ ∆L — the Alexander polynomial is the zeta function of the link exterior. Concretely τHopf ≐ 1 and τBor ≐ (t₁−1)(t₂−1)(t₃−1), with μ̄(123) its leading Taylor coefficient at (1,1,1).
Iwasawa Main Conjecture: the characteristic ideal of the cyclotomic Iwasawa module — the knot-side Λ = Zℓ[[T]] story, see the fS tooltip in the Dictionary — is generated by a p-adic L-function, f ≐ Lp (Mazur–Wiles) — the arithmetic Alexander polynomial is, up to units, a zeta function.

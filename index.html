Knots & Primes

Linking, triple linking, and zeta functions — on both sides of the mirror. Dotted terms carry definitions — hover, Tab, or click to pin; Enter/Space pins too, Esc closes.

Knot theory and number theory run on parallel tracks: primes behave like knots, quadratic residues like linking numbers, and zeta functions arise on both sides by the same sum-into-product mechanism. One story — intersection counts, what happens when they vanish, and the zeta functions that record it all — told simultaneously in both languages, using the smallest genuine examples throughout: the Hopf link and the pair (5, 13), the unlink and the pair (5, 29); the Borromean rings and the triple (13, 61, 937).

Each prime is secretly a circle: the Galois group of the finite field Fp is Ẑ — exactly the profinite fundamental group of the circle S¹ — so Spec Fp is a circle in disguise. And the space it sits in, Spec Z, behaves 3-dimensionally — étale cohomological dimension 3. That is the load-bearing claim of this whole app, so here is what stands behind it, in the open rather than under a cursor. Artin–Verdier duality (1964): on the étale site of Spec OK there is a trace isomorphism H³(Spec OK, Gm) ≅ Q/Z, and the pairing of Hⁱ(F) against Ext³⁻ⁱ(F, Gm) into Q/Z is perfect for constructible F. That is Poincaré duality in top dimension 3, with Gm as the orientation sheaf — and that is the precise sense in which Spec Z behaves like a closed oriented 3-manifold. Two pieces of fine print, since a manifold theorist will ask: Spec Z is not compact, and the real place contributes 2-torsion corrections; both are handled by compactifying Spec Z (adding the archimedean place). Cited, not computed — Artin–Verdier, Woods Hole 1964; Mazur, Ann. Sci. ENS 6 (1973). A circle inside a 3-space is a knot — Mazur's observation, and the reason the table below is a dictionary rather than a coincidence.

The congruence p ≡ 1 (mod 4) tames ramification at 2 and ∞ in the quadratic resolvents — it plays the role of orientability. The taming does not propagate up the tower — the why-box below spells out the S ∪ {∞} convention. One asymmetry worth noting: a knot bounds many Seifert surfaces and every construction must be checked independent of the choice; the quadratic resolvent is unique, so on the arithmetic side well-definedness is automatic.
Over Q there is no rank-r tower mirroring the knot side's Zr-cover. For ℓ ∉ S, the Galois group of the maximal abelian ℓ-extension of Q unramified outside S ∪ {∞} is ∏ᵢ Z/ℓeᵢ with eᵢ = v_ℓ(pᵢ − 1) — finite; for S = {13, 61}, ℓ = 2 it is Z/4 × Z/4. The ∪ {∞} is a convention that must be said, and it bites exactly at ℓ = 2: complex conjugation lands on the order-2 element (2,2), so the totally real (∞-unramified) tower is the strictly smaller Z/4 × Z/2 — p ≡ 1 (mod 4) tames ∞ for the resolvent Q(√p) only, not higher (the quartic subfield of Q(ζ₁₃) is already imaginary). So fS lives over the finite deck group's ring — Z_ℓ[[u₁,…,u_r]] with (1+uᵢ)^(ℓ^eᵢ) = 1 imposed — while Iwasawa's own Λ = Z_ℓ[[T]] series runs over the cyclotomic tower, whose deck group really is Z_ℓ, but which is ramified at ℓ (Morishita, Knots and Primes, ch. 9–12).
One thread, told twice.
An intersection count (linking number / Legendre symbol), symmetric because its two values are the two ends of a single global object (an arc / an adèlic product). When the count vanishes everywhere, the intersection object closes up (a closed curve / the Rédei extension) and its interaction with the third participant becomes the triple invariant (μ̄(123) / [p₁,p₂,p₃]) — well defined exactly because the lower level vanished. And a polynomial (ΔL / fS) records the hierarchy as its surviving low-order coefficients, while being — by Milnor–Turaev and the Main Conjecture respectively — the zeta function of the relevant cover: a sum over iterates or ideals, resummed as a product over orbits or primes, eigenvalues or characters — the pairs the ζ ↔ Δ boxes track, tab by tab, to the Zeta tab.

Topology

lk(K₁,K₂) = signed count of punctures of K₂ through a Seifert surface Σ₁ spanning K₁. Arrowheads mark the orientations — the same parametrization every signed count in this app uses. Design rule, stated once: crossing signs are read in the screen's y-down (left-handed) frame, and the app only ever displays diagram counts that survive that choice — parities and zero totals, never a bare handed sign.
lk = 0
 
Take two Seifert surfaces Σ, Σ′ for K₁. Reverse the orientation of the second and glue: Σ ∪ (−Σ′) has no boundary — the two copies of K₁ cancel — so it is a closed surface in S³. K₂ is not disjoint from that closed surface — it punctures both halves, which is the whole point of the count. What is true is homological: every closed surface in S³ is null-homologous ([Σ ∪ (−Σ′)] = 0 in H₂(S³) = 0), and the algebraic intersection number of a closed curve with a null-homologous closed surface is 0. So K₂ meets Σ ∪ (−Σ′) zero times algebraically. That difference of the two puncture counts is therefore zero: both surfaces give the same lk. The orientation reversal is the whole argument — glue them the same way round and the boundaries add instead of cancelling, and nothing closes up.

Arithmetic

(p₁/p₂) asks an elementary question: is p₁ a nonzero square mod p₂? +1 if yes, −1 if not — computable by hand via Euler's criterion, p₁^((p₂−1)/2) mod p₂, which is exactly what the table below computes. The puncture question is its Galois restatement: "does p₂ puncture k₁ = Q(√p₁)?" becomes "what does Frobenius do?" — (p₁/p₂) = Frobp₂(√p₁)/√p₁.
 
lk ∈ Z but (p₁/p₂) ∈ {±1}: a curve can wind any number of times around a surface, while a degree-2 extension admits only one nontrivial Frobenius. The Legendre symbol is a mod-2 linking number.
Symmetry: one proposition, two proofs, one mechanism

lk(K₁,K₂) = lk(K₂,K₁), and (p₁/p₂) = (p₂/p₁). Both proofs are instances of a single principle: a global object's local contributions must cancel. The two quantities being compared appear as the two ends of one connected global object — an arc, or an adèlic product — and the global object's closedness forces the ends to agree.

What the polynomials say at this stage
Every link L carries a computable polynomial invariant, the Alexander polynomial ΔL(t₁,…,tr): one variable per component, defined up to a unit. Here ΔHopf(t₁,t₂) ≐ 1 — a unit, as trivial as a polynomial can be. For the split unlink, Δ ≐ 0: the classical splitness test. Once lk is known, there is nothing further of low order to report.
With only Gal(k₁/Q) ≅ Z/2 in play, the Iwasawa module — the arithmetic side's H₁-of-the-cover, whose order polynomial fS plays the role ΔL just played — carries nothing beyond the Legendre symbol itself. This changes completely when the pairwise invariants vanish — lk = 0 across the board, all symbols +1.

Because every pairwise lk = 0, each Seifert surface Σᵢ can be chosen disjoint from the other two components (punctures come in cancelling pairs; tube them away). Then Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂ has no boundary at all — it survives as a closed curve — and its intersection with Σ₃, rather than its boundary, becomes the well-defined count: μ̄(123) = Σ sign(p) over p ∈ (Σ₁∩Σ₂) ∩ Σ₃, independent of all choices — Cochran 1990; Mellor–Melvin 2003; mod 2, Morishita ch. 4. (Milnor 1957 defines μ̄ instead as a Magnus coefficient of the longitude; that these two readings agree is exactly what those papers prove — and this app computes both.) The independence holds because the pairwise linking vanished first. For the Borromean rings the three surfaces meet in a single triple point.

Everything the previous tabs computed is packaged by a zeta function on each side, and the packaging is the same: a sum, resummed as a product, in two different ways. Primes play the role of the periodic orbits of Spec(OK) — the Euler product, via Weil's zeta of varieties over finite fields, is what Artin–Mazur imitated in naming the dynamical zeta — and characters diagonalize Frobenius exactly as eigenvalues diagonalize the monodromy.

Which zeta is which: three distinct objects appear on this page and they are related by analogy, not equality — the Lefschetz (dynamical) zeta of a monodromy on the left, the Dedekind zeta of a number field on the right, and, at the bottom, the Alexander polynomial and Iwasawa polynomial, each of which equals a zeta of its own cover up to units (Milnor–Turaev; the Main Conjecture). What the two columns share is the mechanism: one sum, two product resummations. The gallery below then works the zeta at every level of this app's story — two, three and four components, each link paired with its mirror field, plus the half-built five-component rung (one column computed, the other an honest blank), closing with a contrast pair for what knotting, as opposed to linking, would add. (At K = Q everything collapses to Riemann's ζ, e.g. ζ(2) = π²/6: the zeta of the empty link, before any primes are distinguished.)

Lefschetz zeta of a monodromy h : F → F

Sum over iterates
ζh(t) = exp( Σn≥1 L(hⁿ)·tⁿ/n ),  where L(hⁿ) := tr(hⁿ∗ on H₁) — the tr(Aⁿ) of the figure-eight card below; sign convention in the tooltip
Product over eigenvalues of h∗ on H₁(F;Q)
ζh(t) = ∏j (1 − λjt)⁻¹ = det(I − t·h∗)⁻¹
The resummation engine, shown once: −log(1 − x) = Σn≥1 xⁿ/n, so for one eigenvalue exp( Σn λⁿtⁿ/n ) = (1 − λt)⁻¹; summing the exponents over eigenvalues is allowed because tr(hⁿ∗) = Σj λjⁿ. Every sum-into-product identity on this page, in both columns, is this one line — run per eigenvalue, per orbit, or per prime (log of an Euler factor: Σn Np⁻ⁿˢ/n).
Product over periodic orbits (h Anosov on the closed torus, both eigenvalues positive — the demo's bookkeeping)
(1−t)²·ζh(t) = ∏γ (1 − t|γ|)⁻¹  (why (1−t)²? the H₀/H₂ bookkeeping — checked coefficient by coefficient in the figure-eight card of the contrast row below; and γ here indexes periodic orbits, not the Triples curve γ = Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂)
For a fibered knot, Milnor's theorem reads ζLef(t) ≐ ΔK(t)/(1 − t) — Alexander polynomial in the numerator — with ΔK(t) = det(tI − h∗). In the H₁-convention above that is ζh ≐ ΔK⁻¹; the convention bridge and the orbit-face bookkeeping live in the tooltips beside each face.

Fourteen questions a skeptical manifold theorist asks. Every answer either jumps into a tab of this app where the claim can be recomputed on the spot, or carries a name and a year — and theorem, computed live, and analogy never trade places.

1. Why should a prime resemble a knot at all — what is the mathematical content, as opposed to the poetry?
Two theorems and one leap, kept strictly apart. Theorem: Gal(F̄p/Fp) ≅ Ẑ, topologically generated by Frobenius — the same profinite group as the profinite π₁ of the circle — so Spec Fp is a K(Ẑ, 1) exactly as S¹ is a K(Z, 1). Theorem: Spec Z behaves three-dimensionally — étale cohomological dimension 3, with Artin–Verdier duality (Artin–Verdier 1964; Mazur 1973) playing its Poincaré duality. Analogy: "a circle sitting in a 3-space is a knot" — Mazur's observation (unpublished notes, c. 1963–64; Morishita 2012) — asserts no embedding and no isotopy; that leap is the dictionary. The why-box at the top of the states exactly this split, and every later tab inherits it.

2. What plays the role of the ambient S³, and how good is the fit?
Spec Z compactified by the archimedean place — Spec Z ∪ {∞}. The fit rests on two theorems: Artin–Verdier duality in top degree 3 as the Poincaré duality (Artin–Verdier 1964), and π₁ét(Spec Z) = 1 — Q admits no unramified extension (Minkowski 1891) — which is what selects S³, a simply connected closed 3-manifold, rather than an arbitrary one — simple connectivity selecting S³ among closed 3-manifolds by Perelman's proof of the Poincaré conjecture (2003), the one theorem this dictionary borrows from the manifold side of the mirror. The seams show at ∞: it is a place, not a point, and its handling is bookkeeping — the p ≡ 1 (mod 4) "orientability" convention, which you can watch trivialize the v = 2 and v = ∞ rows of the Hilbert-symbol table on the , computed live. And nothing arithmetic plays metric, triangulation or geometrization: the fit is cohomological, not geometric — see the breakage list that closes this tab.

3. What replaces π₁ and covering spaces — and is the Galois correspondence on the arithmetic side literally a theorem?
Literally a theorem — on each side separately. Covers of a space are classified by subgroups of π₁, index = sheet count, normal subgroups ↔ Galois covers (covering-space theory; Hatcher 2002, §1.3); field extensions are classified by subgroups of the Galois group with the identical dictionary (Galois; profinite case Krull 1928), and Grothendieck made the parallel literal: unramified extensions are the finite étale covers of Spec OK, classified by a genuine π₁ét (SGA 1, 1971). Only the pairing of the two columns is analogy. The app draws it box for box: the cover towers on , and each sit opposite their field tower — same lattice, captions carrying these citations.

4. You keep saying "sheets over a prime" — what is a sheet, concretely?
A prime of OK lying above p — a point of the fiber of Spec OK → Spec Z over (p). For unramified p, the theorem e·f·g = [K : Q] with e = 1 (standard; Neukirch 1999, I §8) says the fiber has g points of residue degree f — precisely a loop's preimage in a [K : Q]-sheeted cover: |G|/d components, each winding d times. The app computes this bookkeeping at every level: 2 primes above 61 in Q(√13) (rung 1 of the ), f·g = 8 at eleven primes, 2 among them (∞ is unramified too, but it is not one of the eleven rows) — in the k₁₃,₆₁ table on the , 32 orbits of size 2 above 449 in the degree-64 field. On both sides |G| = count × size, and the caption under each cover tower runs the identity live.

5. Frobenius — why is it "the loop around the prime" and not just an automorphism you happen to like?
Because the arithmetic circle has exactly one loop and Frobenius is it: Gal(F̄p/Fp) ≅ Ẑ is topologically generated by x ↦ xp (theorem), so the generator of π₁ of the "circle" Spec Fp is Frobenius by definition, not by choice. For an unramified prime in a Galois extension, Frobp — defined up to conjugacy — is that loop's monodromy on the cover, the exact analogue of a meridian acting on sheets by deck transformations, conjugacy ambiguity included: basepoint choice on one side, choice of prime above p on the other. The app hands you the loop as a matrix: ρ(Frobq) ∈ N₃(F₂) is computed live for any odd unramified q on the , and at the smallest level the Pairs ζ ↔ Δ box shows Frobenius fixing or swapping the two roots of x² − p mod q — the roots themselves computed by Tonelli–Shanks.

6. Everything in this app is mod 2 — essential, or just the smallest case you could get away with?
The smallest honest case — with one essential asymmetry underneath it. The knot side has the full integers (lk ∈ Z, μ̄ ∈ Z; the Magnus coefficients are computed over Z before any reduction), while Gal(Q(√p)/Q) has order 2, so the Legendre symbol is genuinely a mod-2 linking number — the "Why is the value group only {±1}?" why-box on makes the down-conversion explicit. The framework generalizes: ℓ-th power residue symbols and mod-ℓ Milnor invariants run the same ladder (Morishita 2012, ch. 8), though Rédei's construction itself is quadratic — and ℓ = 2 keeps the deck groups at orders 2, 8, 64, small enough for the app to power every matrix literally. What is essential rather than convenient: over Q no Zr deck group ramified only at the pi — unramified outside them, like the covers opposite — exists: the whyFS box on the computes the fS deck group finite (Z/4 × Z/4 for S = {13, 61}, ℓ = 2) — so an integer-valued arithmetic linking number has nowhere ramified-only-along-the-link to live. The one Zp-tower Q does have — the cyclotomic one the knotted-prime card runs on — ramifies at p itself (fracture 4 below).

7. Why the Borromean rings, and why (13, 61, 937) — are these examples cherry-picked?
Minimal, which is different from cherry-picked. The rings are the smallest instance of the phenomenon at issue: every pairwise lk = 0 — computed from the drawn crossings in Triples step 1 — yet the three are inseparable, μ̄(123) = ±1 is the first surviving invariant, and the longitude is the commutator [x₁, x₂] in Milnor's model (Milnor 1957). The triple (13, 61, 937) meets the mirror specification: all ≡ 1 (mod 4) (the orientability convention), all three pairwise Legendre symbols +1 — the arithmetic unlink, computed live — and Rédei symbol [13, 61, 937] = −1, computed live through the normalized certificate (23, 6, 1); it is the standard small example of the literature (Morishita 2012, ch. 4). Smallness is also policy: the house rule that every number be recomputed at load wants primes a browser handles in milliseconds — the whole computation, Tonelli–Shanks included, runs before your eyes on .

8. The app says μ̄(123) = lk(Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂, K₃) — is that a theorem I can check, or app-local folklore?
A theorem: Cochran 1990 and Mellor–Melvin 2003 (mod-2 version in Morishita 2012, ch. 4), cited at the exact point of use — rung 3 of the — while the app computes μ̄(123) by an independent route, the Magnus coefficient of X₁X₂ in the longitude, so the two readings can be compared on screen. The intersection-theory content in two sentences: with all pairwise lk = 0 each Seifert surface can be pushed off the other two components, so Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂ survives as a closed curve γ, and in general position the triple points of Σ₁, Σ₂, Σ₃ are exactly the points of γ ⋔ Σ₃ — whose signed count is lk(γ, K₃) by definition, since ∂Σ₃ = K₃. Changing any Σᵢ changes the count by an intersection against a closed surface, which vanishes against curves disjoint from it — the pairwise vanishing is the well-definedness hypothesis, and the whyMu box on Triples runs the tubing argument in full.

9. You display two zeta functions side by side and clearly want me to think they are the same object — are they?
No — and the tab says so before you can object: the "which zeta is which" note at the top of the declares the Lefschetz zeta of a monodromy and the Dedekind zeta of a number field related by analogy, not equality, with a third pair — the Alexander and Iwasawa polynomials — each equal to a zeta of its own cover up to units. What the two columns share is a mechanism: one sum resummed as a product two ways — iterates, eigenvalues, orbits on the left; ideals, characters, primes on the right — every instance reduced to −log(1 − x) = Σ xⁿ/n in the "resummation engine" note. The two up-to-units identities are theorems, each within one side of the mirror: τL ≐ ΔL (Milnor 1962; Turaev 1986) and the Main Conjecture f ≐ Lp (Mazur–Wiles 1984; ℓ = 2 by Greither 1992). Equality is only ever asserted within a column — as when ζQ(√5)(2) is computed three ways (ideal sum, Euler product, character product) and converges to the same decimals, live, in the gallery.

11. What is the Iwasawa polynomial really, and what licenses calling an irregular prime "knotted"?
It is the Alexander construction run one ring over. ΔK is the order of H₁ of the infinite cyclic cover as a module over Z[t±1] (deck group Z); Iwasawa's characteristic polynomial is the order of the Galois group of the maximal abelian unramified pro-p tower over Q(ζp∞) as a module over Λ = Zp[[T]] (deck group Zp, T ↔ t − 1) — the parallel is Mazur's (unpublished notes, c. 1963–64; Morishita 2012, ch. 12), quoted on the knotted-prime card. "Knotted" means the polynomial is nontrivial, λ > 0, and the licensing chain is theorem-grade: Kummer's criterion (1850) reduces regularity to Bernoulli numerators, Iwasawa's theorem (1956) forces λ = 0 for regular p, and Herbrand–Ribet (1932/1976) attaches each Bk ≡ 0 (mod p) to a Galois eigenspace on the class group of Q(ζp). The app runs the Bernoulli test live on the : 101 — one of Amano's quadruple primes — is irregular at B₆₈, hence knotted, while its co-stars test regular; 8081 is checked on demand, chunked so the page stays responsive.

12. Your orbit tables smell of Chebotarev — is that theorem hiding in here?
In plain sight. Chebotarev's density theorem (Chebotarev 1926) — Frobenius classes equidistribute over the conjugacy classes of Gal(K/Q) with density |C|/|G| — is the quantitative form of "primes are the periodic orbits," the Zeta tab's opening claim. Its finite shadow is the : every odd unramified q receives a matrix in N₃(F₂) ≅ D₄, and Chebotarev promises the identity with density 1/8 (primes splitting completely, like 107) and the central corner matrix with density 1/8 (partners of the pair at the triple level, like 937) — feed the input box your own primes and watch the sampling. The app exhibits instances only: verifying a density needs primes to infinity, which no browser computes — so here the dictionary's dynamical reading is a proved theorem on the arithmetic side, and the app is the demonstration bench, not the proof.

13. What would a five-component story need?
Topologically, nothing new: a 5-component Brunnian link with longitude ℓ₅ = [[[x₁,x₂],x₃],x₄], whose μ̄(12345) is the coefficient of X₁X₂X₃X₄ — the app's Magnus machinery already truncates at degree 5 and could evaluate it today, and the deck group climbs to N₅(F₂), order 2¹⁰ = 1024, the 's next matrix. Arithmetically: five primes with every pairwise, triple and quadruple symbol equal to +1, plus a well-defined quintuple symbol reading splitting in a degree-1024 field — and there this app's sources stop: Amano's quadruple (Amano, Tohoku Math. J. 66, 2014), computed on , is the frontier, and each new level needs a fresh independence theorem (the analogue of Amano's total-positivity and 2-adic normalization of θ, step 3 there) before the bracket is a function of the primes alone. The ladder predicts the shape; the arithmetic existence-and-well-definedness theorem is the missing rung — a clean statement of where the analogy waits on a theorem.

14. Where does the dictionary break — give me the failure list, not the sales pitch?
Five fractures, each flagged in the app at its point of use. (1) The archimedean place: arithmetic statements carry S ∪ {∞} bookkeeping everywhere — the whyFS box on the shows it biting at ℓ = 2, where the totally real tower drops to Z/4 × Z/2 — while the knot side has no mirror for ∞ beyond the p ≡ 1 (mod 4) orientability convention. (2) No arithmetic hyperbolic geometry: the figure-eight complement carries volume and dilatation φ² (the contrast card), and no prime carries anything like a volume — entropy ↔ λ-invariant is offered there as analogy only. (3) The B₄ gap: the quadruple topology is computed at the Milnor-model level, not from the drawn diagram — the Quadruples honesty note owns it. (4) The ring mismatch: ΔL lives over Z[t₁±1, …, tr±1] with deck group Zr, while over Q no Zr-cover exists — fS's deck group is finite and Iwasawa's genuine Zp-tower ramifies at p itself (whyFS box again) — so the two polynomials live over honestly different rings. (5) It is not a functor: no construction sends links to prime sets or morphisms to morphisms — the dictionary matches invariants and mechanisms, never objects, and asserts no composition or naturality anywhere.
Every answer points back into the app — theorem, computation, or analogy, always labeled which.
What a green suite does and does not mean. The computational checks recompute the load-bearing numbers from scratch — symbols, roots, Magnus coefficients, matrix orders, residue degrees, Bernoulli residues — and would fail if that arithmetic broke. They do not cover every digit on screen: a few displayed quantities (individual digits of the ζ truncations, some term counts) are asserted only to a tolerance, or not at all. The rendered-text pins assert that the prose on the page still matches what the code produces — they catch drift between the two, and nothing else: if code and prose are wrong together, or a theorem is misattributed, or a citation carries the wrong year, this suite stays green. It tests consistency, not truth.

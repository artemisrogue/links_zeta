# The Presentation

*A graduate student presents "Knots & Primes — Both Sides of the Mirror" (`index.html`) to Professor John Klein. The professor works in manifold theory — covering spaces, monodromy, surgery, Poincaré duality are native ground; knot polynomials and algebraic number theory are not assumed. The app is open on the seminar-room screen. Everything the student clicks below is really in the app; every number quoted really renders from a live computation.*

---

**Student.** Before I click anything, the one-sentence thesis: there is a dictionary under which the linking behavior of circles in S³ and the splitting behavior of primes over Q are the same bookkeeping — not metaphorically, but entry for entry, and deep enough that both sides package it into a zeta function the same way. The app's rule is that every number on screen is computed at load, in the page, and everything that is *not* computed is labeled with a name and a year. When you ask "is that a theorem or a slogan," the answer is supposed to already be on the screen.

**Klein.** Then start with the slogan, because I've heard it: "a prime is a knot." A prime is a maximal ideal. In what sense is it a circle?

**Student.** *(Dictionary tab, opens the why-box.)* Two theorems and one leap, and the app keeps them apart. Theorem one: the absolute Galois group of **F**_p is Ẑ, topologically generated by Frobenius. That is the profinite completion of π₁(S¹) — so Spec **F**_p is a K(Ẑ, 1) exactly as the circle is a K(**Z**, 1). For a homotopy theorist that is not poetry; that is the étale homotopy type. Theorem two: Spec **Z** behaves three-dimensionally — étale cohomological dimension 3, and there's a trace isomorphism H³ ≅ **Q**/**Z** with a perfect pairing above it. That's Artin–Verdier duality; the tooltip states it as Poincaré duality in top degree 3 with **G**_m as the orientation sheaf.

**Klein.** So the "3" is cohomological. Fine. And the leap?

**Student.** The leap is the sentence "a circle sitting in a 3-space is a knot." No embedding is constructed, no isotopy category exists on the arithmetic side. That leap is Mazur's, from unpublished notes around 1963, and the app labels it *analogy* everywhere it appears. What makes it worth a seminar rather than a bumper sticker is that the *consequences* of the leap — linking numbers, Milnor invariants, Alexander polynomials, zeta factorizations — all have arithmetic counterparts that are theorems, and the app computes both columns next to each other.

**Klein.** Show me the smallest one. What is the linking number of two primes?

**Student.** *(Pairs tab.)* Take two knots. lk(K₁, K₂) has many faces; the one that transports is: take the double cover of the K₁-complement — the cover classified by π₁ → H₁ → **Z**/2, the meridian to 1. Ask what K₂ does in it. If lk is even, K₂'s class dies, and K₂ lifts to two disjoint loops. If lk is odd, it lifts to one loop double-covering K₂. That's the coset argument, and this figure — *(points at the lift diagram: two sheets, a 2:1 arrow, two loops on the left panel, one doubled loop on the right)* — is exactly that statement.

Now the right column. For a prime p ≡ 1 mod 4, the double cover of Spec **Z** ramified only at p is Spec of the ring of integers in **Q**(√p) — degree 2, one branch point, like a double cover branched over the knot. Ask what a second prime q does in it: it either splits into two primes — two sheets, two fixed points of Frobenius — or stays inert, one prime with residue degree 2 — one orbit of size 2. Which case happens is decided by the Legendre symbol (p/q), which the app computes by Euler's criterion in front of you. Split ↔ unlinked, inert ↔ linked, and the symbol is literally the mod-2 linking number of the two primes in Mazur's dictionary.

**Klein.** Wait. lk is symmetric in the two components. Your symbol (p/q) has an order to it.

**Student.** Caught the right thing — the app refuses to hide it. *(Clicks the (3, 7) preset.)* When both primes are 3 mod 4, (p/q) = −(q/p), and the banner withholds the linking gloss entirely: it says the Euler factor is an honest fact about q's splitting in one specific field, but no symmetric "linking" reading is asserted. The repair is quadratic reciprocity — the symbols agree exactly when at least one prime is 1 mod 4, which is the arithmetic orientability convention: p ≡ 1 mod 4 is what makes the cover unramified at the archimedean place. That's the first crack in the mirror, and the app's policy is to display the crack rather than caulk it.

**Klein.** The archimedean place being the thing your S³ story has no room for.

**Student.** Correct, and it's item one on the failure list — the Q&A tab ends with five fractures, stated as bluntly as I can write them. I'll come back to it. But first the part I most want you to see, because it's intersection theory, which is your home field.

*(Triples tab. Scrolls to "Climbing the ladder with surfaces.")*

The Borromean rings: three circles, pairwise linking zero — computed from the drawn crossings, there, lk = 0 for all three pairs — yet inseparable. Question: if the pairwise invariant vanishes, what is the *next* invariant, and what does it count? The ladder answers in three rungs, surfaces on the left rail, primes on the right.

Rung one. A Seifert surface Σ₁ spans K₁; K₂ punctures it twice, once each way, net zero. That vanishing has an arithmetic mirror: (13/61) = +1, computed live — 61 splits in **Q**(√13). No obstruction at the pairwise level, either column.

Rung two — this is the rung the whole app turns on. Because the punctures cancel, the intersection Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂ has no boundary ends left: in general position it's a *closed curve* γ. The figure draws Σ₂ as a bowl on K₂ dipping through the disk Σ₁ — the crossing circle is γ. γ is not a component of the link. It's new — the object the vanishing left behind. And the arithmetic column manufactures its mirror: because (13/61) = +1, the form x² − 13y² − 61z² has a zero, the certificate is (23, 6, 1) — the app checks 23² − 13·36 = 61 in one line — giving α = 23 + 6√13, and the field k₁₃,₆₁ = **Q**(√13, √61, √α). Rédei's 1939 theorem, cited with a proof sketch in the tooltip: that's a degree-8 dihedral extension ramified only at 13 and 61, independent of the certificate. The dictionary entry is: **γ ↔ √α**. The curve the surfaces cut out corresponds to the element whose square root the vanishing lets you adjoin.

**Klein.** Hold on. "In general position Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂ is a closed curve" — closed, yes, once the boundary intersections cancel. But you drew *one* curve. And well-definedness: γ depends on the surfaces.

**Student.** Both objections are in the app. The why-box under the banner runs the tubing argument: with all pairwise lk = 0 you can choose each Σᵢ disjoint from the other two *components* — punctures come in cancelling pairs; tube them off. For the Borromean rings one circle suffices, and the figure is labeled *schematic* — an honest cartoon of the general-position picture, not a computed embedding. As for well-definedness — that's rung three. The invariant is μ̄(123) = lk(γ, K₃), equivalently the signed count of triple points of Σ₁, Σ₂, Σ₃. Changing any surface changes the count by an intersection against a *closed* surface, which vanishes precisely because the pairwise linking already did. The lower vanishing *is* the well-definedness hypothesis — that's Milnor's 1957 theorem, and the identification of μ̄ with the triple-point count is Cochran 1990 and Mellor–Melvin 2003, cited at the point of use with the two-sentence intersection argument in the tooltip.

**Klein.** So μ̄ is your invariant. Where does the app get its number, if not from surfaces?

**Student.** From a second, independent route — group theory. K₃'s longitude in π₁ of the complement of the other two is the commutator [x₁, x₂] of the meridians. Push it through the Magnus embedding x ↦ 1 + X into noncommuting power series; the coefficient of X₁X₂ is μ̄(123). The app expands it live — coefficient 1. And the arithmetic mirror of that same computation: the Rédei symbol [13, 61, 937], which asks whether 937 splits completely in k₁₃,₆₁. The app runs Tonelli–Shanks for √13 mod 937, evaluates both conjugates of α, and gets −1. Pairwise all +1, triple −1: no two linked, all three linked. Arithmetic Borromean rings.

**Klein.** And the covering space this is all secretly happening in? You keep saying "splits in the degree-8 field" — what's the topological side of *that* sentence?

**Student.** *(Scrolls to the tower.)* Exactly the right demand, and here's the picture I'd draw for you anyway: the tower of covers of S³ ∖ (K₁ ∪ K₂). Bottom X; the two meridian double covers; their (Z/2)² join; and on top an 8-sheet cover with deck group N₃(**F**₂) — upper unitriangular 3×3 over **F**₂, which is D₄. It's the mod-2 Heisenberg cover: π₁ surjects onto the Heisenberg group, meridians to the generators, and the tooltip has the obstruction-theory sketch — N₃ is the central extension of **F**₂² classified by the cup product x₁ ∪ x₂, and that class *is* lk mod 2, so the lift exists exactly because the pairwise linking vanished. Purely topological construction — named for its deck group, not for Rédei. The field k₁₃,₆₁ opposite is its arithmetic mirror, not its definition; the analogy is Morishita's.

And by the Galois correspondence — literal covering-space theory on the left, literal Galois theory on the right — the lattice of intermediate covers and the lattice of intermediate fields are the *same lattice*, the subgroup lattice of D₄, drawn box for box. This is the picture I trust you to check faster than I can present it.

**Klein.** *(a beat)* The lattice is right. So your Rédei symbol is monodromy: [13, 61, 937] is asking what the Frobenius loop at 937 does in the 8-sheet cover.

**Student.** That is the whole dictionary in one sentence, and you just said it, not me. The caption does the counting: μ̄ = 1 puts ρ(K₃) at the order-2 corner of the deck group, so K₃'s preimage upstairs is 8/2 = 4 loops, each winding twice. Opposite: Frobenius at 937 has order 2 in Gal(k₁₃,₆₁/**Q**), so the 8 points upstairs fall into 4 orbits of size 2 — four primes above 937, residue degree 2. Loops × winding = |deck| on the left; g × f = [k : **Q**] on the right. Same arithmetic, two categories.

**Klein.** Where's the payoff? Counting loops in a cover is Tuesday morning. You promised zeta functions.

**Student.** *(Zeta tab.)* The payoff is that both columns package those counts identically, and the gallery works it out at every level — two, three, four components, each link paired with its field, every card a live computation. The row heads are the story: **two** — Hopf link against **Q**(√5); **three** — Borromean against k₁₃,₆₁; **four** — Milnor's link B₄ against a degree-64 field.

Two components first, because it's checkable in your head. *(Opens the Hopf card.)* The Hopf link is fibered — annulus fiber, monodromy trivial on H₁ — so its zeta is det(tI − h₁) = t − 1: nothing there but linking, honestly. The cover-orbit face is the dial: lk odd means K₂ lifts to one doubled loop, factor (1 − t²)⁻¹, and opposite it, (5/13) = −1 computed live: 13 inert in **Q**(√5), Euler factor (1 − 13⁻²ˢ)⁻¹. Unlink, and it's (1 − t)⁻² against 29 split, (1 − 29⁻ˢ)⁻². *(Opens the **Q**(√5) card.)* Then the whole Dedekind zeta of **Q**(√5) is those dials multiplied out — the app computes ζ at s = 2 three ways, ideal sum, Euler product, character product, and you watch the three decimals converge to each other as you drag the cutoff.

**Klein.** Three ways being your sum-over-iterates, product-over-eigenvalues, product-over-orbits.

**Student.** Precisely the three faces, and the left column states them for a monodromy with the one-line engine −log(1 − x) = Σxⁿ/n that powers every identity on the page. Now three components, since you asked for a payoff. *(Opens the k₁₃,₆₁ card.)* Here is a degree-8 Dedekind zeta *worked*: eleven local factors, each read off the order of a matrix — the app powers ρ(Frob_p) in N₃(**F**₂) live for each row — f = 1 at 107, which splits completely; f = 4 at 2 and 7 and 11; and at 937, the triple symbol's −1 forces the factor **(1 − 937⁻²ˢ)⁻⁴**. Then it multiplies the product out at s = 2 over the 44 unramified primes up to 200: 1.067395, six decimals, live, with an honest clause that the two ramified factors are omitted. And directly opposite — *(opens the Borromean worked card)* — the link's own zeta: exp(Σ 4tⁿ/n) = (1 − t)⁻⁴ verified coefficient by coefficient, 1, 4, 10, 20, 35 — binomials, polynomial growth, no spectrum — plus the cover-orbit factor (1 − t²)⁻⁴, which the card is careful to call a *different object* than the monodromy zeta: fiber homology versus covering-space orbits. The twinning is between (1 − t²)⁻⁴ and 937's (1 − 937⁻²ˢ)⁻⁴. One bookkeeping.

**Klein.** You said four components. Four-fold linking that every pair and every triple misses — and you can *hear* it in a zeta function?

**Student.** In a degree-64 field. Amano's theorem — Tohoku 2014, cited on the banner with its independence statement — builds K with Galois group N₄(**F**₂), and the quadruple symbol [5, 8081, 101, 449] reads whether 449 splits completely. The app computes all six pairwise symbols and all four triple symbols: +1 across the board — 449 is invisible at every lower level. The quadruple: −1. Frobenius sits at the corner of N₄(**F**₂), order 2, and the 64 points upstairs fall into **32 orbits of size 2**: local factor (1 − 449⁻²ˢ)⁻³². The order rule itself — order 4 iff N² ≠ 0, with the three surviving entries written out — is *brute-forced over all 64 group elements in front of you*; the card prints "rule checked over all 64 elements: OK" because it just did. Topologically: Milnor's model B₄, longitude [[x₁,x₂],x₃], Magnus coefficient computed live, ρ(K₄) at the corner, 32 loops of double length in the 64-sheet cover — the twin factor (1 − t²)⁻³².

**Klein.** "Milnor's model." The picture on your Quadruples tab is a specific four-component diagram. Did you compute its Wirtinger presentation, or the model's?

**Student.** The model's — and the app says so in a boxed honesty note rather than in a footnote you'd need to find: μ̄(1234) is computed for Milnor's B₄, the commutator archetype; the drawn diagram's own Wirtinger presentation is not implemented. It's fracture three on the failure list. I'd rather show you the gap than have you find it.

**Klein.** Good. That's the first thing today I'd have written a referee report about, and you defused it. Now the criticism you can't defuse so easily. Your two zeta columns. The left one is a Lefschetz zeta of a diffeomorphism. The right one is a Dedekind zeta. You've shown me matching *factors*. Are you claiming a common object — some space whose zeta specializes to both?

**Student.** No — and the tab's second paragraph says so before any card opens: *analogy, not equality*. There is no flow on Spec O_K in this app and no claim of one. What is asserted is: the same three-face mechanism, instance by instance; and *within* each column, genuine theorems that a polynomial equals a zeta of its own cover up to units — Milnor–Turaev on the left, τ_L ≐ Δ_L, and the Iwasawa Main Conjecture on the right, f_Λ ≐ L_p, Mazur–Wiles for odd ℓ and Greither for ℓ = 2, which is the prime this entire app runs on. Those two theorems are the load-bearing wall: each column separately says "my polynomial is a zeta." The dictionary matches the columns; it never merges them.

**Klein.** Then here's my constructive version. If Δ is the zeta of the infinite cyclic cover, the object underneath is the Alexander *module* — H₁ of the cover as a **Z**[t, t⁻¹]-module — and on the other side the Iwasawa *module* over Λ. Your app shows me the polynomials. The modules are where a topologist would live: torsion, presentation matrices, the chain complex. If you want this to teach *me*, put the module structure on screen — even one presentation matrix.

**Student.** Accepted, genuinely — that's the right next build. Two things exist today as down payments: the Milnor–Turaev tooltip states the mechanism at the chain level — the twisted Wirtinger boundary map *is* the Alexander matrix, which is why the torsion is the polynomial — and the knotted-prime card names the Iwasawa side's module over Λ = **Z**_p[[T]] with T ↔ t − 1 explicitly. But displaying an actual presentation matrix, side by side — that goes on the list, with your name on the request.

**Klein.** Second criticism. Your rung figures are cartoons — you said so. For a talk, fine. For teaching, I'd want one *computed* picture: take your drawn Borromean crossings, build the Seifert matrices, and let the app verify γ's existence numerically instead of schematically.

**Student.** Also fair, also on the list — the ladder's caption is explicit that the numbers are live but the embeddings are not. The honest current state is: computed numbers, cartoon geometry, and every cartoon labeled as such.

**Klein.** Last question, and it's the one I came in with. Why should any of this be more than a coincidence factory? Give me one thing the dictionary *predicted* that surprised you.

**Student.** *(Opens the contrast row.)* The contrast pair is my answer. Everything so far was linking — and linking, the left column shows, lives at t = 1: links of unknots like these have unipotent monodromy, all eigenvalues 1, zeta coefficients growing polynomially, the entire content sitting in Taylor coefficients at 1, which are exactly the μ̄ hierarchy. So the dictionary asks: what is the arithmetic mirror of *knotting* — of eigenvalues pushed off 1? The figure-eight knot's monodromy is Anosov: dilatation φ², entropy log φ² ≈ 0.962, traces 3, 7, 18, 47 growing exponentially — the app checks its zeta faces coefficient by coefficient too. Mazur's answer: a prime has its own tower — the cyclotomic **Z**_p-tower — and its own Alexander-type polynomial there, the Iwasawa polynomial. Regular primes have trivial polynomial: unknotted. Irregular primes are the knotted ones. The app runs the Bernoulli test live — von Staudt–Clausen legitimizes the mod-p computation, tooltip with the counting proof — and here is the thing that surprised me, which I did not plant: **101, one of Amano's own four primes, is irregular** — B₆₈ ≡ 0 mod 101, computed in four milliseconds at load. The app's cast contained a knotted prime all along, and the linking story never noticed, because knotting and linking are independent dials — different covers of different things. The figure-eight card and the regular-vs-irregular card sit opposite each other saying exactly that.

**Klein.** *(looks at the 101 row for a moment)* That's a good jewel. It's the right kind of surprise — the dictionary generated a question you wouldn't have asked in one column alone. All right. Summary judgment: the covering-space spine is solid and honestly labeled; the towers are the best slide; the model-gap and the cartoons are owned rather than hidden, which I respect; the module structure is missing and you know it. What's the thesis problem?

**Student.** *(Q&A tab, last two items.)* Five components. Topologically it's already in reach — the Magnus machinery here truncates at degree 5, the deck group is N₅(**F**₂), order 1024. Arithmetically, nobody has the quintuple analogue of Amano's independence theorem — the normalization that makes the bracket a function of the five primes alone. The ladder predicts the shape of the missing rung; proving it exists is the thesis. The app ends by saying that out loud — right after the failure list, which I'll leave on screen, because you taught me that the failure list is the most convincing slide in any deck.

**Klein.** It usually is. Send me the link — and next time, presentation matrices.

The ladder, live: Frobenius as a matrix

For the pair (13, 61) of the Triples tab, every odd prime q ∤ 13·61 gets an actual matrix ρ(Frobq) ∈ N₃(F₂): the superdiagonal entries are its two Legendre symbols (0 for +1, 1 for −1), and the corner is its splitting in the Rédei field k₁₃,₆₁ — defined only when the superdiagonal vanishes, because only then is the corner independent of the choices (why?). Everything below is computed live: two Euler-criterion symbols, and — when both vanish — Tonelli–Shanks for √13 mod q and the Legendre symbol of α = 23 + 6√13 mod q. The definedness domain, precisely: the corner — the splitting of q in the field k₁₃,₆₁ itself, fixed once and for all — is defined for every odd unramified q once the superdiagonal vanishes, whatever q is mod 4. The [13, 61, q] is the same number when moreover q ≡ 1 (mod 4): that extra congruence buys the symbol its symmetry and reciprocity — what makes it a triple linking number, matching μ̄ — not the definedness of the corner. So the Rédei-symbol routine rejects q ≡ 3 (mod 4) while this matrix does not; the self-tests pin both behaviors at q = 107, by design.

 
How often does each matrix appear? — the statistics under the whole app

Every prime you feed the box above lands on one of D₄'s five conjugacy classes — identity (splits completely, like 107), corner (the triple level, like 937), or one of the three χ-patterns. Chebotarev's density theorem (1926, cited) says each class captures its fair share of all primes, |C|/|G|: here 1/8, 1/8, 1/4, 1/4, 1/4. This is the engine room of two things this app does: it is why the meets local factors of each shape in fixed proportions as the cutoff grows, and it is the quantitative face of the Dictionary's "primes are the periodic orbits" — orbits of a flow equidistribute; Frobenius classes do too (the 's Chebotarev answer states the analogy's limits). The bench demonstrates at a finite cutoff; the theorem is about the limit.

Gal(k₁₃,₆₁/Q) ≅ D₄ ⊂ N₃(F₂); Gal(K/Q) ≅ N₄(F₂) for Amano's quadruple. The ladder is the mirror, group by group.

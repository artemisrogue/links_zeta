Fourteen questions a skeptical manifold theorist asks. Every answer either jumps into a tab of this app where the claim can be recomputed on the spot, or carries a name and a year — and theorem, computed live, and analogy never trade places.

1. Why should a prime resemble a knot at all — what is the mathematical content, as opposed to the poetry?
Two theorems and one leap, kept strictly apart. Theorem: Gal(F̄p/Fp) ≅ Ẑ, topologically generated by Frobenius — the same profinite group as the profinite π₁ of the circle — so Spec Fp is a K(Ẑ, 1) exactly as S¹ is a K(Z, 1). Theorem: Spec Z behaves three-dimensionally — étale cohomological dimension 3, with Artin–Verdier duality (Artin–Verdier 1964; Mazur 1973) playing its Poincaré duality. Analogy: "a circle sitting in a 3-space is a knot" — Mazur's observation (unpublished notes, c. 1963–64; Morishita 2012) — asserts no embedding and no isotopy; that leap is the dictionary. The why-box at the top of the states exactly this split, and every later tab inherits it.

2. What plays the role of the ambient S³, and how good is the fit?
Spec Z compactified by the archimedean place — Spec Z ∪ {∞}. The fit rests on two theorems: Artin–Verdier duality in top degree 3 as the Poincaré duality (Artin–Verdier 1964), and π₁ét(Spec Z) = 1 — Q admits no unramified extension (Minkowski 1891) — which is what selects S³ rather than an arbitrary closed 3-manifold. Mind the gap in that step: π₁ét is the profinite completion, so π₁ét = 1 says only that there are no finite covers. Upgrading that to genuine simple connectivity needs residual finiteness of 3-manifold groups (Hempel 1987) — a second borrowed theorem, on top of Perelman's proof of the Poincaré conjecture (2003), which is what makes simple connectivity select S³ among closed 3-manifolds. Those two are what this dictionary borrows from the manifold side of the mirror. The seams show at ∞: it is a place, not a point, and its handling is bookkeeping — the p ≡ 1 (mod 4) "orientability" convention, which you can watch trivialize the v = 2 and v = ∞ rows of the Hilbert-symbol table on the , computed live. And nothing arithmetic plays metric, triangulation or geometrization: the fit is cohomological, not geometric — see the breakage list that closes this tab.

3. What replaces π₁ and covering spaces — and is the Galois correspondence on the arithmetic side literally a theorem?
Literally a theorem — on each side separately. Covers of a space are classified by subgroups of π₁, index = sheet count, normal subgroups ↔ Galois covers (covering-space theory; Hatcher 2002, §1.3); field extensions are classified by subgroups of the Galois group with the identical dictionary (Galois; profinite case Krull 1928), and Grothendieck made the parallel literal: unramified extensions are the finite étale covers of Spec OK, classified by a genuine π₁ét (SGA 1, 1971). Only the pairing of the two columns is analogy. The app draws it box for box: the cover towers on , and each sit opposite their field tower — same lattice, captions carrying these citations.

4. You keep saying "sheets over a prime" — what is a sheet, concretely?
A prime of OK lying above p — a point of the fiber of Spec OK → Spec Z over (p). For unramified p in a Galois extension K/Q, the theorem e·f·g = [K : Q] with e = 1 (standard; Neukirch 1999, I §8 — outside the Galois case only Σᵢ eᵢfᵢ = [K : Q] holds, the primes above p need not share a residue degree) says the fiber has g points of residue degree f — precisely a loop's preimage in a [K : Q]-sheeted cover: |G|/d components, each winding d times. The app computes this bookkeeping at every level: 2 primes above 61 in Q(√13) (rung 1 of the ), f·g = 8 at eleven primes, 2 among them (∞ is unramified too, but it is not one of the eleven rows) — in the k₁₃,₆₁ table on the , 32 orbits of size 2 above 449 in the degree-64 field. On both sides |G| = count × size, and the caption under each cover tower runs the identity live.

5. Frobenius — why is it "the loop around the prime" and not just an automorphism you happen to like?
Because the arithmetic circle has exactly one loop and Frobenius is it: Gal(F̄p/Fp) ≅ Ẑ is topologically generated by x ↦ xp (theorem), so the generator of π₁ of the "circle" Spec Fp is Frobenius by definition, not by choice. For an unramified prime in a Galois extension, Frobp — defined up to conjugacy — is that loop's monodromy on the cover, the exact analogue of a meridian acting on sheets by deck transformations, conjugacy ambiguity included: basepoint choice on one side, choice of prime above p on the other. The app hands you the loop as a matrix: ρ(Frobq) ∈ N₃(F₂) is computed live for any odd unramified q on the , and at the smallest level the Pairs ζ ↔ Δ box shows Frobenius fixing or swapping the two roots of x² − p mod q — the roots themselves computed by Tonelli–Shanks.

6. Everything in this app is mod 2 — essential, or just the smallest case you could get away with?
The smallest honest case — with one essential asymmetry underneath it. The knot side has the full integers (lk ∈ Z, μ̄ ∈ Z; the Magnus coefficients are computed over Z before any reduction), while Gal(Q(√p)/Q) has order 2, so the Legendre symbol is genuinely a mod-2 linking number — the "Why is the value group only {±1}?" why-box on makes the down-conversion explicit. The framework generalizes: ℓ-th power residue symbols and mod-ℓ Milnor invariants run the same ladder (Morishita 2012, ch. 8), though Rédei's construction itself is quadratic — and ℓ = 2 keeps the deck groups at orders 2, 8, 64, small enough for the app to power every matrix literally. What is essential rather than convenient: over Q no Zr deck group ramified only at the pi — unramified outside them, like the covers opposite — exists: the whyFS box on the computes the fS deck group to be finite (Z/4 × Z/4 for S = {13, 61}, ℓ = 2) — so an integer-valued arithmetic linking number has nowhere ramified-only-along-the-link to live. The one Zp-tower Q does have — the cyclotomic one the knotted-prime card runs on — ramifies at p itself (fracture 4 below).

7. Why the Borromean rings, and why (13, 61, 937) — are these examples cherry-picked?
Minimal, which is different from cherry-picked. The rings are the smallest instance of the phenomenon at issue: every pairwise lk = 0 — computed from the drawn crossings in Triples step 1 — yet the three are inseparable, μ̄(123) = ±1 is the first surviving invariant, and the longitude is the commutator [x₁, x₂] in Milnor's model (Milnor 1957). The triple (13, 61, 937) meets the mirror specification: all ≡ 1 (mod 4) (the orientability convention), all three pairwise Legendre symbols +1 — the arithmetic unlink, computed live — and Rédei symbol [13, 61, 937] = −1, computed live through the normalized certificate (23, 6, 1); it is the standard small example of the literature (Morishita 2012, chs. 4/8). Smallness is also policy: the house rule that every number be recomputed at load wants primes a browser handles in milliseconds — the whole computation, Tonelli–Shanks included, runs before your eyes on .

8. The app says μ̄(123) = lk(Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂, K₃) — is that a theorem I can check, or app-local folklore?
A theorem: Cochran 1990 and Mellor–Melvin 2003 (mod-2 version in Morishita 2012, chs. 4/8), cited at the exact point of use — rung 3 of the — while the app computes μ̄(123) by an independent route, the Magnus coefficient of X₁X₂ in the longitude, so the two readings can be compared on screen. The intersection-theory content in two sentences: with all pairwise lk = 0 each Seifert surface can be pushed off the other two components, so Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂ survives as a closed curve γ, and in general position the triple points of Σ₁, Σ₂, Σ₃ are exactly the points of γ ⋔ Σ₃ — whose signed count is lk(γ, K₃) by definition, since ∂Σ₃ = K₃. Changing any Σᵢ changes the count by an intersection against a closed surface, which vanishes against curves disjoint from it — the pairwise vanishing is the well-definedness hypothesis, and the whyMu box on Triples runs the tubing argument in full.

9. You display two zeta functions side by side and clearly want me to think they are the same object — are they?
No — and the tab says so before you can object: the "which zeta is which" note at the top of the declares the Lefschetz zeta of a monodromy and the Dedekind zeta of a number field related by analogy, not equality, with a third pair — the Alexander and Iwasawa polynomials — each equal to a zeta of its own cover up to units. What the two columns share is a mechanism: one sum resummed as a product two ways — iterates, eigenvalues, orbits on the left; ideals, characters, primes on the right — every instance reduced to −log(1 − x) = Σ xⁿ/n in the "resummation engine" note. The two up-to-units identities are theorems, each within one side of the mirror: τL ≐ ΔL (Milnor 1962; Turaev 1986) and the Main Conjecture f ≐ Lp (Mazur–Wiles 1984; ℓ = 2 by Greither 1992). Equality is only ever asserted within a column — as when ζQ(√5)(2) is computed three ways (ideal sum, Euler product, character product) and converges to the same decimals, live, in the gallery.

10. What exactly is computed live in this app, and what am I being asked to take on faith?
The house discipline: every displayed number is computed at runtime unless it carries a "cited, not computed" label where it appears, and every structural identification is cited by name and year at its point of use — nothing is silently both. The labelled exceptions are few and deliberate: h(Q(√5)) = 1, ΔHopf, ΔBor, the "all other places" Hilbert row, and λ = 1 for 37. Computed: all Legendre symbols (Euler's criterion), Tonelli–Shanks square roots, Rédei symbols with Rédei's normalization enforced, Magnus coefficients, matrix orders by literal powering, every zeta-coefficient identity checked face against face, Bernoulli numbers mod p. Cited: fiberedness (Stallings 1978), μ̄(123) = lk(γ, K₃) (Cochran 1990; Mellor–Melvin 2003), the model longitudes (Milnor 1957), Rédei's ramification statement (Rédei 1939), Amano's quadruple-symbol theorem, Thm 3.2.5 under hypotheses (3.2.1) (Amano, Tohoku Math. J. 66, 2014), Milnor–Turaev, Mazur–Wiles and Greither. One gap recurs, and is flagged everywhere it applies: the Milnor invariants are computed for Milnor's model words rather than from the drawn diagrams, because no Wirtinger presentation is implemented. That is μ̄(123) at step 2, μ̄(1234) at , and μ̄(12345) in the five-component Zeta card — each flagged where it appears — the first two beside their pictures, the third in the equation line of a card that has no figure to sit beside. The self-test suite in the footer pins both kinds, recomputing the numbers and asserting the prose against the rendered page — press Run self-tests and read the list.

11. What is the Iwasawa polynomial really, and what licenses calling an irregular prime "knotted"?
It is the Alexander construction run one ring over. ΔK is the order of H₁ of the infinite cyclic cover as a module over Z[t±1] (deck group Z); Iwasawa's characteristic polynomial is the order of the Galois group of the maximal abelian unramified pro-p tower over Q(ζp∞) as a module over Λ = Zp[[T]] (deck group Zp, T ↔ t − 1) — the parallel is Mazur's (unpublished notes, c. 1963–64; Morishita 2012, ch. 12), quoted on the knotted-prime card. "Knotted" means the polynomial is nontrivial, λ > 0, and the licensing chain is theorem-grade: Kummer's criterion (1850) reduces regularity to Bernoulli numerators, Iwasawa's theorem (1959) forces λ = 0 for regular p, and Herbrand–Ribet (1932/1976) attaches each Bk ≡ 0 (mod p) to a Galois eigenspace on the class group of Q(ζp). The app runs the Bernoulli test live on the : 101 — one of Amano's quadruple primes — is irregular at B₆₈, hence knotted, while its co-stars test regular; 8081 is checked on demand, chunked so the page stays responsive.

12. Your orbit tables smell of Chebotarev — is that theorem hiding in here?
In plain sight. Chebotarev's density theorem (Chebotarev 1926) — Frobenius classes equidistribute over the conjugacy classes of Gal(K/Q) with density |C|/|G| — is the quantitative form of "primes are the periodic orbits," the Zeta tab's opening claim. Its finite shadow is the : every odd unramified q receives a matrix in N₃(F₂) ≅ D₄, and Chebotarev promises the identity with density 1/8 (primes splitting completely, like 107) and the central corner matrix with density 1/8 (partners of the pair at the triple level, like 937) — feed the input box your own primes and watch the sampling. The app exhibits instances only: verifying a density needs primes to infinity, which no browser computes — so here the dictionary's dynamical reading is a proved theorem on the arithmetic side, and the app is the demonstration bench, not the proof.

13. What would a five-component story need?
Topologically, nothing new: a 5-component Brunnian link with longitude ℓ₅ = [[[x₁,x₂],x₃],x₄], whose μ̄(12345) is the coefficient of X₁X₂X₃X₄ — the app's Magnus machinery already truncates at degree 5 and could evaluate it today, and the deck group climbs to N₅(F₂), order 2¹⁰ = 1024, the 's next matrix. Arithmetically: five primes with every pairwise, triple and quadruple symbol equal to +1, plus a well-defined quintuple symbol reading splitting in a degree-1024 field — and there this app's sources stop: Amano's quadruple (Amano, Tohoku Math. J. 66, 2014), computed on , is the frontier, and each new level needs a fresh well-definedness theorem (the analogue of Amano's hypotheses and certificate conditions of θ, step 3 there) before the bracket is a function of the primes alone. The ladder predicts the shape; the arithmetic existence-and-well-definedness theorem is the missing rung — a clean statement of where the analogy waits on a theorem.

14. Where does the dictionary break — give me the failure list, not the sales pitch?
Five fractures, each flagged in the app at its point of use. (1) The archimedean place: arithmetic statements carry S ∪ {∞} bookkeeping everywhere — the whyFS box on the shows it biting at ℓ = 2, where the totally real tower drops to Z/4 × Z/2 — while the knot side has no mirror for ∞ beyond the p ≡ 1 (mod 4) orientability convention. (2) No arithmetic hyperbolic geometry: the figure-eight complement carries volume and dilatation φ² (the contrast card), and no prime carries anything like a volume — entropy ↔ λ-invariant is offered there as analogy only. (3) The B₄ gap: the quadruple topology is computed at the Milnor-model level, not from the drawn diagram — the Quadruples honesty note owns it. (4) The ring mismatch: ΔL lives over Z[t₁±1, …, tr±1] with deck group Zr, while over Q no Zr-cover exists — fS's deck group is finite and Iwasawa's genuine Zp-tower ramifies at p itself (whyFS box again) — so the two polynomials live over honestly different rings. (5) It is not a functor: no construction sends links to prime sets or morphisms to morphisms — the dictionary matches invariants and mechanisms, never objects, and asserts no composition or naturality anywhere.
Every answer points back into the app — theorem, computation, or analogy, always labeled which.

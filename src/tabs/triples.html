Because every pairwise lk = 0, each Seifert surface Σᵢ can be pushed off the other two components — the punctures come in cancelling pairs, so they tube away. Note what this does and does not give: Σᵢ misses the other curves, not the other surfaces. Making Σ₁ and Σ₂ disjoint as well — while both still miss K₃ — would force μ̄(123) = 0, and the Borromean rings have μ̄(123) = ±1. That failure is the whole content of this rung. (Pairwise-disjoint surfaces for all components would make it a boundary link, and those have every Milnor invariant zero — but that is a condition on all three at once, not on one pair.) Then Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂ has no boundary at all — it survives as a closed curve — and its intersection with Σ₃, rather than its boundary, becomes the well-defined count: μ̄(123) = Σ sign(p) over p ∈ (Σ₁∩Σ₂) ∩ Σ₃, independent of all choices — Cochran 1990; Mellor–Melvin 2003; mod 2, Morishita chs. 4/8, 1st ed. (Milnor 1957 defines μ̄ instead as a Magnus coefficient of the longitude; that these two readings agree is exactly what those papers prove — and this app computes both.) The independence holds because the pairwise linking vanished first. For the Borromean rings the three surfaces meet in a single triple point.

The Rédei symbol is the triple Massey product ⟨χ₁, χ₂, χ₃⟩ in Galois cohomology, defined precisely because the pairwise cup products (Legendre symbols) vanish first — and, by Morishita's theorem (Compositio Math. 140, 2004), it equals the arithmetic triple Milnor invariant μ̄₂(123), read from the pro-2 Magnus expansion of Frobenius exactly as μ̄(123) is read from the longitude. The topological side's coefficient statement is computed live at left. The matching statement for fS — that it reports the Rédei symbol once every lower-order term vanishes — is offered as analogy, not theorem: over Q the deck group is finite (see the fS why-box under the Dictionary table). The two sides say the same sentence, one as computation, one as analogy: when the cheap invariants vanish, the polynomial's leading surviving coefficient is the triple invariant.
Vanishing is not emptiness — it is what makes the next invariant well defined.

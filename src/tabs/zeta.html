Everything the previous tabs computed is packaged by a zeta function on each side, and the packaging is the same: a sum, resummed as a product, in two different ways. Primes play the role of the periodic orbits of Spec(OK) — the Euler product, via Weil's zeta of varieties over finite fields, is what Artin–Mazur imitated in naming the dynamical zeta — and characters diagonalize Frobenius exactly as eigenvalues diagonalize the monodromy.

Which zeta is which: three distinct objects appear on this page and they are related by analogy, not equality — the Lefschetz (dynamical) zeta of a monodromy on the left, the Dedekind zeta of a number field on the right, and, at the bottom, the Alexander polynomial and Iwasawa polynomial, each of which equals a zeta of its own cover up to units (Milnor–Turaev; the Main Conjecture). What the two columns share is the mechanism: one sum, two product resummations. The gallery below then works the zeta at every level of this app's story — two, three and four components, each link paired with its mirror field, plus the half-built five-component rung (one column computed, the other an honest blank), closing with a contrast pair for what knotting, as opposed to linking, would add. (At K = Q everything collapses to Riemann's ζ, e.g. ζ(2) = π²/6: the zeta of the empty link, before any primes are distinguished.)

Lefschetz zeta of a monodromy h : F → F

Sum over iterates
ζh(t) = exp( Σn≥1 L(hⁿ)·tⁿ/n ),  where L(hⁿ) := tr(hⁿ∗ on H₁) — the tr(Aⁿ) of the figure-eight card below; sign convention in the tooltip
Product over eigenvalues of h∗ on H₁(F;Q)
ζh(t) = ∏j (1 − λjt)⁻¹ = det(I − t·h∗)⁻¹
The resummation engine, shown once: −log(1 − x) = Σn≥1 xⁿ/n, so for one eigenvalue exp( Σn λⁿtⁿ/n ) = (1 − λt)⁻¹; summing the exponents over eigenvalues is allowed because tr(hⁿ∗) = Σj λjⁿ. Every sum-into-product identity on this page, in both columns, is this one line — run per eigenvalue, per orbit, or per prime (log of an Euler factor: Σn Np⁻ⁿˢ/n).
Product over periodic orbits (h Anosov on the closed torus, both eigenvalues positive — the demo's bookkeeping)
(1−t)²·ζh(t) = ∏γ (1 − t|γ|)⁻¹  (why (1−t)²? the H₀/H₂ bookkeeping — checked coefficient by coefficient in the figure-eight card of the contrast row below; and γ here indexes periodic orbits, not the Triples curve γ = Σ₁ ∩ Σ₂)
For a fibered knot, Milnor's theorem reads ζLef(t) ≐ ΔK(t)/(1 − t) — Alexander polynomial in the numerator — with ΔK(t) = det(tI − h∗). In the H₁-convention above that is ζh ≐ ΔK⁻¹; the convention bridge and the orbit-face bookkeeping live in the tooltips beside each face.

Dedekind zeta of a number field K

Sum over ideals
ζK(s) = Σa N(a)⁻ˢ
Euler product over primes
ζK(s) = ∏p (1 − Np⁻ˢ)⁻¹
Product over characters (Gal(K/Q) abelian)
ζK(s) = ∏χ L(s, χ)
For K = Q(√p₁, √p₂), the character product is four Dirichlet L-functions, and the nontrivial ones encode exactly the Legendre symbols of the Pairs tab.

The contrast pair what knotting — as opposed to linking — adds: the spectral dial, on both sides

Contrast: what knotting adds — the figure-eight knot — eigenvalues pushed off 1: the spectral dial
Lesson (links ↔ primes): knotting moves the spectrum — dilatation φ², entropy log φ² > 0 — and its arithmetic mirror is the irregular prime in the card opposite, whose Iwasawa polynomial gains genuine degree. Linking touches neither spectrum.
4₁ is also a fibered knot — but knotted: its monodromy acts on H₁ by the Anosov matrix A = [[2,1],[1,1]] with dilatation λ = (3+√5)/2 — eigenvalues pushed off 1, unlike the unipotent Borromean cards in the three-component row above. Both H₁-faces of ζh and the orbit face (Artin–Mazur, = (1−t)²·ζh) are verified below, coefficient by coefficient.
Milnor's theorem, live: det(tI − A) = t² − 3t + 1 = Δ4₁(t) — the knot's zeta is its Alexander polynomial. Honesty note: the fiber is a once-punctured torus; the fixed-point counts above are taken for the same matrix on the closed torus (Fix = |det(Aⁿ − I)| = tr(Aⁿ) − 2), where the orbit bookkeeping is cleanest — the H₁-action, all the faces use, is identical. The orbit-face check verifies the exponential–product identity plus integrality of the Möbius counts — whole-number orbit counts are the statement that iterates organize into closed orbits — the same orbit bookkeeping as the Pairs box.

The polynomials close both loops
Milnor–Turaev: the Milnor–Reidemeister torsion of the Zr-cover satisfies τL ≐ ΔL — the Alexander polynomial is the zeta function of the link exterior. Concretely τHopf ≐ 1 and τBor ≐ (t₁−1)(t₂−1)(t₃−1), with μ̄(123) its leading Taylor coefficient at (1,1,1).
Iwasawa Main Conjecture: the characteristic ideal of the cyclotomic Iwasawa module — the knot-side Λ = Zℓ[[T]] story, see the fS why-box under the Dictionary table — is generated by a p-adic L-function, fΛ ≐ Lp (Mazur–Wiles; ℓ = 2 by Greither; the subscript Λ keeps this characteristic power series apart from the residue degree f in the local-factor tables above) — the arithmetic Alexander polynomial is, up to units, a zeta function.
Torsion orders the class number, and the one place the mirror is visibly not symmetric

The topological class number — branched cyclic covers of the trefoil — and one of them is the Poincaré sphere
Lesson (links ↔ primes): |H₁| of a cover is the topological class number, and here it is computed from the same determinant the card opposite the figure-eight reads as a fixed-point count — one quantity, two readings.
Let Mn be the n-fold cyclic cover of S³ branched over the trefoil. Fox's formula gives |H₁(Mn)| as the resultant of Δ with tn − 1. The trefoil is fibered with monodromy A = [[1, −1], [1, 0]], so the resultant collapses to |det(An − I)| = |2 − tr(An)|, and tr(An) obeys sn = sn−1 − sn−2 read straight off Δ3₁ = t² − t + 1. Integer arithmetic throughout — no floating point.
These are the Brieskorn manifolds Σ(2, 3, n) — homology spheres exactly at the gcd(n, 6) = 1 rows, which here are n = 5 and n = 7. Of those, Σ(2, 3, 5) is the Poincaré homology sphere.

The class number — and what this app can and cannot compute of it — h itself is cited; p | h is computed
Lesson (links ↔ primes): Cl(K) ↔ H₁(M) is the dictionary entry; h = 1 mirrors "homology sphere". But the two sides are not equally answerable, and that asymmetry is the point of this row.
Under the standard correspondence (Mazur; Morishita ch. 3) the ideal class group Cl(K) plays the part of H₁(M, Z), so the class number h = |Cl(K)| is the arithmetic order opposite. Cited, not computed: class numbers are outside this app's engine — the same limit Amano step 0 hits when it needs h(Q(√5)) = 1. What this app does compute is a divisibility: by Kummer's criterion, p | h(Q(ζp)) exactly when p is irregular — which is the Bernoulli computation the regular-vs-irregular card above runs live, and why 101 is flagged there.
Where the mirror stops being symmetric. Topologically we can say exactly which of these covers have class number one — those with gcd(n, 6) = 1, checked live opposite. Arithmetically, "which fields have class number one" is Gauss's class number problem: settled for imaginary quadratics only in the 1960s, and open in general. The dictionary carries the definition across; it does not carry the answer.
